    Section 3.2 Exponential & Logarithmic Rate of Change Formulas

    Subsection 3.2.1 The Formulas

    For exponential and logarithmic functions, we have the following formulas:
    Name Function Derivative
    General Exponential Rule \(f(x) = b^x\) \(f'(x) = \ln b\cdot b^x\)
    Exponential Rule \(f(x) = e^x\) \(f'(x) = e^x\)
    Logarithm Rule \(f(x) = \ln x\) \(f'(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}\)

    Subsection 3.2.2 Examples

    Example 3.2.1.

    Write the formula for the derivative of the function.
    1. \(\displaystyle h(x) = 3-7e^x\)
    2. \(\displaystyle f(x) = 6(0.8)^x\)
    3. \(\displaystyle f(a) = 10\lrpar{1+\dfrac{0.05}{4}}^{4a}\)
    4. \(\displaystyle g(x) = 4\ln x - e^\pi\)
    5. \(\displaystyle f(x) = 3.7e^x - 2\ln x\)
    6. \(\displaystyle y(x) = -\ln x + 2e^x\)
    7. \(\displaystyle f(g) = 4\sqrt{g} + 5(1.2)^g\)
    8. \(\displaystyle k(t) = P\lrpar{1+\dfrac{r}{n}}^{nt}\)
    Solution.
    1. \(\displaystyle h'(x) = -7e^x\)
    2. \(\displaystyle f'(x) = 6\cdot \ln(0.8)\cdot (0.8)^x\)
    3. \(\displaystyle f'(a) = 10\cdot \lrpar{\ln\lrpar{\lrpar{1+\dfrac{0.05}{4}}^4}}\lrpar{\lrpar{1+\dfrac{0.05}{4}}^4}^a\)
    4. \(\displaystyle g'(x) = \dfrac{4}{x}\)
    5. \(\displaystyle f'(x) = 3.7e^x - \dfrac{2}{x}\)
    6. \(\displaystyle y'(x) = -\dfrac{1}{x} + 2e^x\)
    7. \(\displaystyle f'(g) = 2g^{-1/2} + 5\cdot \ln(1.2)\cdot (1.2)^g\)
    8. \(\displaystyle k'(t) = P\cdot \ln\lrpar{\lrpar{1+\dfrac{r}{n}}^n}\cdot \lrpar{\lrpar{1+\dfrac{r}{n}}^n}^t\)

    Example 3.2.2.

    For the first two hours after yeast dough has been kneaded, it doubles in volume approximately every 42 minutes. If 1 quart of yeast dough is left to rise in a warm room, its growth can be modeled as \(v(h) = e^h\) quarts, where \(h\) is the number of hours the dough has been allowed to rise.
    1. How many minutes will it take the dough to attain a volume of 2.5 quarts?
    2. Write a model for the rate of growth of the yeast dough.
    Solution.
    1. 54.96 minutes
    2. \(v'(h) = e^h\) quarts per hour, rate of change of dough growth, \(h\) hours after the dough has been allowed to rise

    Example 3.2.3.

    The weight of a laboratory mouse between 3 and 11 weeks of age can be modeled as \(w(t) = 11.3 + 7.37\ln t\) grams, where the age of the mouse is \(t+2\) weeks.
    1. What is the weight of a 9-week-old mouse? Round to the nearest hundredth.
    2. Write a rate of change model for the weight of the mouse, and determine how rapidly its weight is changing at 9 weeks.
    3. What is the average rate of change in the weight of the mouse between ages 7 and 11 weeks? Round to the nearest hundredth.
    4. Does the rate at which the mouse is growing increase or decrease as the mouse gets older? Why?
    Solution.
    1. 25.64 grams
    2. \(w'(t) = \dfrac{7.37}{t}\) grams per week gives the rate of change of a lab mouse's weight, at age \(t+2\) weeks
      \(w'(7) = 1.05\) grams per week
      The second part gives people fits, because the alignment is unusual; be careful here.
    3. 1.08 grams per week
    4. The rate decreases. The output of the derivative goes down as time increases.

    Example 3.2.4.

    Suppose the managers of a dairy company have modeled weekly production costs as \(c(u) = 3250 + 75\ln u\) dollars for \(u\) units of dairy products. Weekly shipping cost for \(u\) units is given by \(s(u) = 50 u + 1500\) dollars.
    1. Write the formula for the total weekly cost of production and shipping of \(u\) units.
    2. Write the rate of change model of the total weekly cost of producing and shipping \(u\) units.
    3. Calculate the total cost to produce and ship 5000 units in 1 week.
    4. Calculate and interpret the rate of change in the total cost to produce and ship 5000 units in 1 week.
    Solution.
    1. \(\displaystyle t(u) = 4750 + 75\ln u + 50u\)
    2. \(t'(u) =\dfrac{75}{u} + 50\) dollars per unit, rate of change of weekly cos, when \(u\) units are produced.
    3. \(\displaystyle t(5000) = $255388.79\)
    4. When 5000 units are produced, the weekly toatl cost of shipping and production is increasing by 50.02 dollars per unit.

    Example 3.2.5.

    An individual has $45,000 to invest. $32,000 will be put into a low-risk mutual fund averaging 6.2% interest compounded monthly, and the remainder will be invested in a high-yield bond fund averaging 9.7% interest, compounded continuously.
    1. Write an equation for the total amount in the two investments, using \(I(t)\) as your function.
    2. Write the rate of change model for the low-risk fund, using \(L(t)\) as your function.
    3. Write the rate of change model for the high-yield fund, using \(H(t)\) as your function.
    4. Write the rate of change model for the combined investment.
    5. Calculate and interpret \(\dfrac{dI}{dt}\) after 8 months, and after 18 months.
    Solution.
    1. \(I(t) = 32\lrpar{1+\dfrac{0.062}{12}}^{12t} + 13e^{.097t}\) thousand dollars in the two investments, \(t\) years after investment
    2. \(L'(t) = 32\cdot \lrpar{\ln\lrpar{1+\dfrac{0.062}{12}^{12}}}\cdot \lrpar{1+\dfrac{0.062}{12}}^{12t}\) thousand dollars per year, rate of change of the low-risk investment, \(t\) years after investment
    3. \(H'(t) = 13\cdot \ln\lrpar{e^{0.097}}\cdot e^{0.097t}\) thousand dollars per year, rate of change of the high-risk investment, \(t\) years after investment
    4. \(I'(t) = 32\cdot \lrpar{\ln\lrpar{1+\dfrac{0.062}{12}^{12}}}\cdot \lrpar{1+\dfrac{0.062}{12}}^{12t} + 13\cdot \ln\lrpar{e^{0.097}}\cdot e^{0.097t}\) thousand dollars per year, rate of change of total investment, \(t\) years after investment
    5. 8 months after initial investment, the value is increasing by 3.41 thousand dollars per year.
      18 months after initial investment, the value is increasing by 3.63 thousand dollars per year.
