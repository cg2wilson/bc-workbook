    Section 2.3 Rates of Change: Notation & Interpretation

    Subsection 2.3.1 Average ROC vs. Instantaneous ROC

    Average Rate of Change.

    • Measures how rapidly a quantity changes over an interval
    • Slope of the secant line between two points

    (Instantaneous) Rate of Change.

    • Measures how rapidly a quantity is changing at a specific point
    • Slope of the tangent line at a single point
    • Function must be smooth and continuous

    Subsection 2.3.2 Notation and Terminology

    Rate of change at a specific point \(a\) is often referred to as any of the following (given a function \(f\)):
    • the derivative of \(f\) at \(a\)
    • the slope of a graph of \(f\) at point \((a,f(a))\)
    • the slope of the line tangent to a graph of \(f\) at a point \((a,f(a))\)
    • the rate of change of \(f\) at \(a\)
    We also have two different notations for the derivative of \(f\) at point \(a\text{:}\)
    • \(f'(a)\text{.}\) This is read ''\(f\) prime of \(a\)''
    • \(\dfrac{df}{dx}\bigg|_{x = a}\text{.}\) This is read ''d-f d-x, evaluated at \(a\)'', or as ``the derivative of \(f\) with respect to \(x\text{,}\) evaluated at \(a\)'
    Note: We will freely interchange between any of the above terminology or notations.

    Subsection 2.3.3 Examples

    Example 2.3.1.

    The function \(f\) gives weekly profit, in thousands of dollars, that an airline makes on flights from Boston to Washington, D.C. when the ticket price is \(p\) dollars. Write a sentence interpreting the following:
    1. \(\displaystyle f(65)=15\)
    2. \(\displaystyle f'(65)=1.5\)
    3. \(\displaystyle f'(90) = -2\)
    Solution.
    1. When the ticket price for a flight from Boston to DC is $65, the weekly profit is 15 thousand dollars
    2. When the ticket price for a flight from Boston to DC is $65, the weekly profit is increasing by 1.5 thousand dollars (profit) per dollar (ticket price)
    3. When the ticket price for a flight from Boston to DC is $90, the weekly profit is decreasing by 2 thousand dollars (profit) per dollar (ticket price)
    In parts b and c, the parenthetical descriptions aren't explicitly necessary, but might be helpful to make the unit make sense.

    Example 2.3.2.

    The function \(C\) gives the number of bushels of corn produced on a tract of farmland that is treated with \(f\) pounds of nitrogen per acre.
    1. Is it possible for \(C(90)\) to be negative? Why?
    2. What are the units of \(\dfrac{dC}{df}\bigg|_{f=90}\text{?}\)
    3. Is it possible for \(\dfrac{dC}{df}\bigg|_{f=90}\) to be negative? Why?
    4. Give an alternate notation for the statement \(\dfrac{dC}{df}\bigg|_{f=90}\text{.}\)
    Solution.
    1. No. You can't produce negative corn.
    2. bushels of corn per pound of nitrogen (per acre)
    3. Yes. Negative derivative indicates that the rate of change of production is negative, i.e. production is decreasing (but not negative!).
    4. \(\displaystyle C'(90)\)
    Recently I've found myself switching the order of parts b and c; it seems to make more sense.

    Example 2.3.4.

    The function \(w\) gives a certain Business Calculus instructor's weight (in pounds) \(t\) weeks after he begins a diet. Write a sentence of interpretation for each of the following statements:
    1. \(w(0) = 180\) and \(w(12) = 165\)
    2. \(w'(1) = -2\) and \(w'(9) = -1\)
    3. \(\dfrac{dw}{dt}\bigg|_{t=12} = 0\) and \(\dfrac{dw}{dt}\bigg|_{t=15} = 0.25\)
    Solution.
    1. When the diet begins, the Business Calculus instructor weighs 180 pounds.
      12 weeks after the diet began, the Business Calculus instructor weighs 165 pounds.
    2. 1 week after the diet begins, the insructor's weight is decreasing by 2 pounds per week
      9 weeks after the diet begins, the insructor's weight is decreasing by 1 pound per week
    3. 12 weeks after the diet begins, the insructor's weight is not changing
      15 weeks after the diet begins, the instructor's weight is increasing by 0.25 pounds per week

    Example 2.3.5.

    2. \(m'(4)\) is greater than any other slope
    3. \(\displaystyle m'(0) = m'(6) = 0\)
    4. The graph of \(m\) has no direction changes

    Example 2.3.6.

    The function \(g\) gives the fuel efficiency in miles per gallon of a car traveling \(v\) miles per hour. Write a sentence of interpretation for each of the following:
    1. \(g(55)=32\) and \(g'(55)=-0.25\)
    2. \(g'(45) = 0.15\) and \(g'(51) = 0\)
    Solution.
    1. When the car is traveling 55 miles per hour, the fuel efficiency is 32 miles per gallon
      When the car is traveling 55 miles per hour, the fuel efficiency is decreasing by 0.25 miles per gallon per mile per hour
    2. When the car is traveling 45 miles per hour, the fuel efficiency is increasing by 0.15 miles per gallon per mile per hour
      When the car is traveling 51 miles per hour, the fuel efficiency is not changing
    The unit tends to confuse people here, and that might cause some other confusions when writing derivative-style interpretations.
